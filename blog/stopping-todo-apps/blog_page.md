---
title: Why I stopped using to-do apps
date: 2026-04-10
description: I've tried every productivity app out there. I keep coming back to a plain text file.
---

I've used Notion, Todoist, Things, TickTick, and a few others I've already forgotten. Each one started with a week of genuine enthusiasm and ended with me opening it less and less until I quietly stopped.

The problem isn't the apps. They're all fine. The problem is that the overhead of maintaining a system takes energy that should go toward the actual work.

What I do now is keep a single plain text file called `todo.txt`. One line per task. No tags, no priorities, no due dates unless something genuinely has one. When something is done, I delete it.

It sounds worse than the apps. It probably is, by most metrics. But I actually use it, which none of the apps managed to achieve past month two.

## What I keep in it

Just tasks I need to do today or this week. Nothing aspirational, no "someday" lists, no project hierarchies. If something isn't happening soon it doesn't go in the file.

## Why it works for me

The low barrier to entry matters. Opening a text file and typing a line is faster than opening an app, navigating to the right project, filling out a form. Friction kills habits.

Also, when the list gets long enough to be uncomfortable, that discomfort is useful information. With an app I could collapse sections and pretend I had everything under control. With a flat file I can't hide from it.

## What I still use apps for

Calendar for anything time-specific. Notes app for longer-form reference material. The text file is only for action items.

Not trying to convince anyone else to do this. Just the thing that happened to work for me after a lot of trying other things.
