---
title: Notes on learning things faster
date: 2026-03-22
description: Some things I've noticed about how I actually learn, versus how I thought learning worked.
---

School taught me that learning meant reading something, memorizing it, and recalling it on demand. That model mostly works for exams and mostly fails for everything else.

Here's what I've found works better.

## Do the thing first

When I read documentation before touching the code, almost none of it sticks. When I try to build something first, hit a wall, and then read the relevant section, it sticks immediately. The confusion creates a slot for the information to land in.

This is annoying because it means failing more at the start. But it's faster overall.

## Teach it to someone

Not literally, but writing an explanation of something I just learned forces me to find the gaps. I keep a private notes file where I explain things to an imaginary person who knows less than me. The places where the explanation gets vague or hand-wavy are exactly the places I don't actually understand yet.

## Minimize the number of things being learned at once

Every project involves learning something. The mistake I kept making was picking projects that required learning five new things simultaneously. The cognitive load of that means I'm not learning any of them well, just muddling through.

Now I try to keep the unknown surface area small: one new language, one new concept, one unfamiliar domain at a time. Boring constraint, but it works.

## Spaced repetition for things that need to be memorized

For anything genuinely worth memorizing, Anki. Not for everything. Understanding a concept doesn't require memorizing it. But if there's something I want to be able to recall quickly without looking it up, flashcards are better than rereading.

---

None of this is original. Most of it is a rediscovery of things that have been written about extensively. But there's a gap between knowing learning principles abstractly and actually remembering to apply them, which I suppose is its own little irony.
